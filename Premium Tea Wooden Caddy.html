I · The Origin

A garden that grew from a king's quiet dream,
between two snowfields and a slow river.

In 1969, on the gentle slopes that fall from the Tendong hill range, a 440-acre stretch of forest was cleared and planted with tea. The vision came from Palden Thondup Namgyal, the last Chogyal of Sikkim — partly as a working industry for his hill people, partly as a means of resettling Tibetan refugees who had arrived in the years after 1959.

The garden was called Temi. Half a century later, it remains the only tea estate in the state of Sikkim — a single, contiguous garden run by the Government of Sikkim, certified fully organic by Switzerland's Institute of Marketecology in 2008.

The leaf is grown the slow way. Orthodox processing — careful plucking, wilting, rolling, oxidation and drying — on loamy, gently-sloping soil at the foot of the Eastern Himalaya. Around 100 tonnes of finished tea leaves this hillside each year; about three-quarters of it goes to the Kolkata auction.

II · The Cup

Bold but unhurried.
A long, honey-bright finish.

Trace the cup with your tongue and you'll find four moving parts. Hover any of them to read what we tasted on the bench.

Honeyed
Hover any slice
I.
Aroma — delicate, floral
A soft floral lift, the kind sources describe again and again — cool mountain air, a hint of mountain flowers, nothing assertive. The leaf is grown in mist; the cup remembers it.
II.
Body — light, smooth
Orthodox black, but light on the tongue rather than heavy. Round and unhurried; the official estate note simply calls it “a premium grade tea, light in color with a delicate flavor and aroma.”
III.
Finish — lingering, lightly sweet
A clean, faintly muscatel sweetness that settles slowly — the muscatel comparison that tea drinkers reach for when placing Sikkim somewhere near, but distinct from, Darjeeling.
IV.
Liquor — light golden amber
Brews to a bright, translucent liquor — closer to gold than to mahogany. The first flush in particular is described as light, golden yellow with a delicate flavour.

V · Recognition

A small estate with a long memory.
The recognitions, the certifications, the singularity.

1969
A garden begins
Established by Palden Thondup Namgyal, the last Chogyal of Sikkim. The 440-acre garden is planted on the slopes of the Tendong hill range — a working industry, and a way to resettle Tibetan refugees in the region.
1994 · 95
All India Quality Award
The Tea Board of India presents Temi with its All India Quality Award for two consecutive years — 1994 and 1995 — recognising the cup quality of an estate then barely twenty-five years old.
2008
Certified organic
After conversion under the guidelines of Switzerland's Institute of Marketecology (IMO), the whole estate is certified 100% organic. Inspected annually by IMO's Bangalore unit.
2019
Land to the workers
Landholding rights for the homesteads of 192 worker families are distributed by the Government of Sikkim — a continuation of the estate's founding idea: an industry that belongs to the people who tend it.
